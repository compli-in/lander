## Part 5: Controls, Not Policies

SOC 2 does not audit policies.

It audits controls.

Most teams invert this.

They write policies first and assume compliance follows.

It does not.

### What a Control Actually Is

A control is an enforced rule inside your system.

Not a statement of intent.

Examples:

* Access is restricted based on roles  
* Changes require approval workflows  
* Logs are generated and monitored  
* Data is encrypted at rest and in transit  

If the system enforces it, it is a control.

If it is written in a document, it is not.

### What a Policy Actually Is

A policy is a declaration.

It describes what should happen.

It does not guarantee anything happens.

Example:

* “Access is restricted to authorized users”

Without enforcement, this is meaningless.

### Where Companies Fail

Common pattern:

* Policies are written to satisfy audit requirements  
* Controls are loosely implemented or missing  
* Evidence is manually assembled  

This creates a gap:

Policy says one thing.  
System does another.

Auditors test the system.

Not the document.

### The Illusion of Compliance

Policies create false confidence.

Because:

* They are easy to produce  
* They look complete  
* They pass initial review  

But they do not survive testing.

SOC 2 does not reward documentation.

It rewards enforcement.

### Control Design vs Control Operation

Two layers matter:

**Design**  
Does the control exist?

**Operation**  
Does it work consistently over time?

Most teams focus on design.

Auditors focus on operation.

### The Evidence Link

Every control must produce evidence.

Examples:

* Access logs  
* Change approvals  
* Incident records  
* Monitoring alerts  

If a control does not generate evidence, it cannot be proven.

If it cannot be proven, it fails.

### The System Constraint

Controls must be:

* Enforced automatically  
* Observable through logs  
* Consistent across time  

Manual controls degrade.

Inconsistent controls fail.

### The Only Useful Model

Policies describe intent.

Controls enforce reality.

SOC 2 evaluates reality.

If your controls do not exist inside your systems,  
your compliance does not exist either.
